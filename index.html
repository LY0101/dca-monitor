0 · Normal — below the warning threshold
1 · Elevated — first threshold crossed

Shiller CAPE (valuation)
The cyclically-adjusted P/E of the S&P 500 — price ÷ 10-year inflation-adjusted average earnings. Backtest-temp · Adaptive Strategy vs Buy & Hold · experimental / work-in-progress
Apples-to-apples backtest: the adaptive strategy is run fully invested — no cash, no profit-taking sells — so it is directly comparable to always-invested Buy & Hold. Each month the full $10,000 contribution buys the regime's target ETFs (bull/fear → TQQQ+SOXL, chop/euphoria → QQQ+SMH), from the first month all four ETFs existed.
Verdict
Fully invested, the adaptive strategy (53.01×, Sharpe 0.84, -80.3% drawdown) essentially replicated Buy & Hold TQQQ — the regime-switching added no risk-adjusted value. The real win is the annual 25% cap. There are two ways to apply it: (a) trim the excess to cash — safest, drawdown -80.3% → -45.5%, Sharpe 0.93, but return falls to 15.23×. (b) stay fully invested by rebalancing to equal weight — this is the standout: it keeps 51.34× (nearly the full 53.01×) while cutting the drawdown to -59.0% and posting the best Sharpe of any leveraged strategy (0.95). Annually selling the runaway winner and buying the laggards harvests volatility — almost free risk reduction. Unleveraged QQQ still has the highest Sharpe overall (1.08, -32.6% drawdown). Takeaway: a simple annual rebalance — not the regime-switching — is what improves risk-adjusted return, and the fully-invested equal-weight version captures it best.

The goal of this page: turn discipline into a system so it doesn't depend on willpower in the moment.

Sit out just the 10 best and you forfeit 97% of the outcome. The only reliable way to be present for them is to always be invested and always contribute — which is precisely what skipping DCA and panic-selling break. And remember the ride: a -80.3% max drawdown and a -43.2% worst month. Those WILL recur — plan for them now.
Biases → corrections
“I'll wait for a better entry” (market-timing)
How it bites you: The most common DCA killer — skipping months to wait for a dip or 'more clarity.' You end up timing the market (the exact thing DCA removes) and usually buy less, later, and higher.
The fix: Automate it. Set a recurring auto-invest at your broker on a fixed date (say the 1st). Make the monthly buy a default that happens without a decision — willpower is unreliable, a standing order isn't.
Panic-selling in a drawdown (loss aversion)
How it bites you: This strategy has historically fallen ~80% peak-to-trough. In the moment every instinct screams sell — and selling near the bottom turns a paper drawdown into a permanent loss. It's the single biggest gap between backtest and real results.
The fix: Pre-commit on paper, while calm: 'I will not sell leveraged positions in a drawdown; I keep buying on schedule.' Decide your −50% and −80% response NOW, not at the bottom. A written rule beats in-the-moment emotion.
FOMO / doubling down after a run-up
How it bites you: After a big rally it feels safest to add more leverage — exactly when valuations and euphoria signals are highest and forward returns are worst. That's buying the most shares at the worst prices.
The fix: Never increase the leverage sleeve after a strong run. Let the Euphoria and Valuation-Warning flags be your 'stop adding leverage' trigger — new money in those months goes to the unleveraged sleeve, never more 3×.
Checking too often (action bias + anxiety)
How it bites you: A 3× strategy watched daily maximizes stress and the urge to tinker. Most actions taken from daily monitoring are noise that quietly erodes returns.
The fix: Downgrade to a monthly ritual: run the dashboard once a month, act, close it. The framework is monthly by design — daily watching adds anxiety, not return.
Recency bias / overconfidence
How it bites you: The backtest is the best-case decade for leveraged tech, with no secular bear. After wins it's tempting to size up and assume it continues — which is how leverage ruins people in the regime that hasn't happened yet.
The fix: Anchor to the worst case, not the recent past. Size the sleeve so you could survive a near-total loss of it, and rebalance back down after gains (the 25% cap) rather than letting winners run.
Skipping contributions when scared
How it bites you: Fear peaks exactly when prices are lowest — so the months you most want to skip are the months DCA pays off most (Fear I/II buy the cheapest shares of the whole cycle).
The fix: Remove the choice — automate the contribution so fear can't intervene. If you must act manually, schedule the transfer before you read the news, not after.
Mental accounting (judging the sleeve alone)
How it bites you: Looking at the leveraged sleeve in isolation makes an −80% drop feel catastrophic — and that feeling is what triggers the panic sell.
The fix: Always view it as a % of total liquid net worth against your safe T-bill core. A properly-sized sleeve falling 80% is a survivable dent to the whole — that framing is what lets you hold.
Your commitment system (make discipline the default)

☐ Write your drawdown plan before you need it — "−50% and −80% = keep buying, don't sell." Sign it.

💡 Investment Tips · Hard-Won Wisdom
Distilled from strategy reviews — the principles behind this framework, grouped by theme. Revisited periodically as the strategy evolves.
Sizing
Cap the sleeve at ~10–15% of liquid net worth
Position size is the only risk control that always works. Set the cap on PEAK exposure — including any crash doubledown.
Could you survive the sleeve going to zero?
The honest question isn't 'what's my return' — it's whether a near-total loss of the leveraged book leaves your life intact.
Structure
Run a barbell: huge safe core, small hot satellite
Most wealth in T-bills/treasuries, a small slice in leverage. The T-bill fund IS your hedge — held separately, not bolted into the strategy.
Leverage
3× ETFs only work in trends — they bleed in chop
Daily rebalancing causes volatility decay: 15–30%/yr lost in sideways markets even if the index goes nowhere.
Recovery math is brutal — avoid deep drawdowns
From −90% you need +900% to break even; from −97%, +3,200%. Dodging the drawdown beats chasing the upside.
Concentration is hidden: it's all one tech bet
QQQ, TQQQ, SMH, SOXL overlap heavily. Diversify at the TOTAL-portfolio level (the safe core), not inside the sleeve.
Skip the daily circuit breaker
Single-day stops whipsaw viciously on 3× ETFs, and you can't execute intraday on a framework you check once a day.
Timing
VIX is coincident, not predictive
It spikes AFTER price falls. VIX > 45 is not the bottom — in 2008 it hit 45 in September, then the market fell ~30% more into March.
Ladder crash deployment by depth, not a single lump
Don't empty the reserve at the first panic print. Buy progressively more as drawdown deepens so you're never out of ammo at the bottom.
Don't trust correlations in a crisis
In 2022 stocks and long bonds fell together — the classic TQQQ/TMF hedge broke exactly when it was needed.
Euphoria can last for months — don't short the top
Overheated ≠ imminent crash. Pause new buys and trim into strength; never bet against a melt-up with leverage.
CAPE catches valuation bubbles that momentum misses
Shiller CAPE flagged BUBBLE at the 2000 top while VIX/momentum stayed calm. It's the one signal that sees an expensive market at low volatility.
Discipline
Profit-taking sell order: SOXL → TQQQ → SMH → QQQ never
Trim the highest-decay leverage first. QQQ is the permanent core and is never sold.
Judge by MAR ratio, not raw return
CAGR ÷ max drawdown is the real scoreboard. A strategy that returns less but draws down half as much usually wins long term.
Beware the backtest — it only saw the good years
TQQQ/SOXL launched in 2010, the best tech window ever. A 3× Nasdaq through 2000–02 was a near-total loss. Size for the regime you haven't seen.
Hysteresis: slow to get aggressive, fast to get defensive
Confirmation months stop you chasing FOMO into leverage; fear regimes switch immediately. Asymmetry by design.

Volatility
VIX — CBOE Volatility Index
implied volatility
The market's expected 30-day volatility of the S&P 500, implied by options prices. Often called the 'fear gauge' — it rises when investors pay up for downside protection.
How it's measured: Derived live from S&P 500 option premiums. It is forward-looking (what traders EXPECT), not a measure of what already happened.
ValueWhat it means
Below 15Complacency — very calm, little fear priced in
15 – 20Normal bull-market range
20 – 30Chop — rising caution
30 – 45Fear — panic conditions
Above 45Extreme fear — crash / once-per-decade
In this framework: The hard boundary for the regime: <20 bull · 20–30 chop · 30–45 Fear I · >45 Fear II.
Realized Volatility (1-month)
actual volatility
How much the price ACTUALLY moved over the last month — the real-world counterpart to VIX's expectation.
How it's measured: Standard deviation of the last 21 daily log returns, annualized (×√252). Shown as a percentile vs the ticker's full history.
ValueWhat it means
Below 33rd pctileCalm — small actual swings
33rd – 66thNormal historical range
66th – 90thElevated — turbulent
Above 90thExtreme — historic stress
In this framework: A leverage-decay gauge. High realized vol is when 3× ETFs bleed fastest. A big SMH-vs-QQQ gap warns specifically against SOXL.

Key Concepts
Implied vs Realized volatility
VIX is IMPLIED — what option prices say traders expect. Realized vol is ACTUAL — what already happened. When realized is high but VIX is low, the market is moving a lot without pricing in fear (a notable divergence).
Volatility decay (beta slippage)
3× ETFs reset their leverage daily. In choppy markets this 'constant-leverage trap' loses money even if the index ends flat: +25% then −20% returns the index to start, but a 3× fund drops ~30%. This is why the framework avoids leverage in chop and high realized vol.
Percentile
Where today's reading ranks against the metric's entire history. '90th percentile' means it has only been this high 10% of the time — instantly tells you if a value is normal or extreme.
Hysteresis
Confirmation delays that stop the regime flip-flopping on noise. The framework is slow to turn aggressive (bull needs 2 months) and fast to turn defensive (Fear is immediate).
Volatility × Valuation
The key synthesis: high vol + cheap = opportunity (2008); high vol + expensive = bubble bursting (2000); low vol + expensive = complacency/top (2021); low vol + cheap = healthy accumulation.
